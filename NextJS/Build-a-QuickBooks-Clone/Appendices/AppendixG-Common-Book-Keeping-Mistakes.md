## Appendix G: Common Bookkeeping Mistakes and Month-End Close Practices

This appendix covers real-world bookkeeping practice: mistakes beginners commonly make, how to catch them, and the "month-end close" checklist real bookkeepers and accountants run through regularly to keep books accurate and trustworthy.

---

### 1. Mistake: confusing profit with cash in the bank

**The mistake:** Looking at a healthy bank balance and assuming the business is profitable, or looking at a low bank balance and panicking that the business is losing money — without checking the actual P&L.

**Why it happens:** Cash and profit are genuinely different things. A business can have a lot of cash sitting in the bank from a big loan it took out, or from customer deposits not yet earned — none of which is profit.

**How to catch/avoid it:** Always check the actual P&L for profit questions and the Balance Sheet's cash/bank balance for liquidity questions — never substitute one for the other.

### 2. Mistake: recording a loan or equipment purchase as an expense

**The mistake:** Recording the full amount of a loan received, or a large equipment purchase, as Income or Expense respectively.

**Why it's wrong:** A loan received is not income — it's a liability that must be repaid. An equipment purchase is not (usually) an immediate expense — it's an asset that provides value over years. Recording these incorrectly badly distorts the P&L.

**How to catch it:** If a single large transaction causes a dramatic, one-time spike or drop in Net Profit that doesn't reflect normal business activity, investigate it — it's often miscategorized.

### 3. Mistake: not separating business and personal expenses

**The mistake:** Paying for personal expenses out of the business bank account, and recording them as business expenses, or not recording them consistently at all.

**Why it's a problem:** It corrupts the P&L, makes owner's equity tracking inaccurate, and in many jurisdictions creates real tax/legal risk (commingling personal and business finances can even jeopardize liability protections for incorporated businesses).

**How to fix it:** Personal expenses paid from a business account should be recorded as an Owner's Draw, not as a business Expense. The cleanest long-term solution is maintaining genuinely separate bank accounts and cards for business vs. personal use.

### 4. Mistake: not recording accrued expenses/income at period end

**The mistake:** Only recording transactions when a bill/invoice document exists or cash moves, ignoring situations where an expense was clearly incurred by period-end but no formal document/payment happened yet.

**Example:** Employees worked the last week of the month, but payroll won't actually be run/paid until the 5th of next month. If this isn't recorded as an "accrued" expense in the correct month, that month's P&L understates expenses.

**How to handle it:** Accrual adjusting entries at period-end record the expense/income in the period it truly belongs to, with a corresponding liability/receivable, even before the actual bill or payment exists.

### 5. Mistake: leaving transactions "uncategorized" indefinitely

**The mistake:** Importing bank transactions and letting many of them sit in "uncategorized" status for weeks or months.

**Why it's a problem:** Uncategorized transactions don't appear on the P&L or Balance Sheet at all. Reports generated while transactions sit uncategorized are silently incomplete.

**How to catch it:** Regularly check the count of uncategorized transactions and clear the backlog before trusting any report.

### 6. Mistake: double-counting a transaction

**The mistake:** Recording the same transaction twice — e.g., manually entering a bill, then also importing it again later via a bank feed/CSV without noticing it's a duplicate.

**How to catch it:** Look for suspiciously duplicated transaction amounts and dates. A full bank reconciliation process is the most reliable systematic way to catch this.

### 7. Bank reconciliation — the single most powerful error-catching habit

**What it is:** Regularly (ideally monthly) comparing every transaction recorded in the books against the actual bank statement for the same account and period, confirming every transaction on the statement has a matching entry in the books (and vice versa), and that the ending balances agree.

**Why it works so well:** The bank statement is an independent, external source of truth that a business doesn't control — it can't be silently wrong the same way internal bookkeeping might be. Any mismatch surfaces almost every category of common bookkeeping mistake: missed transactions, duplicates, wrong amounts, wrong dates.

### 8. A realistic month-end close checklist

1. **Clear the uncategorized transactions backlog**
2. **Reconcile every bank and credit card account**
3. **Review Accounts Receivable aging** — overdue invoices needing follow-up, or ones to write off as bad debt
4. **Review Accounts Payable aging** — bills that need to be paid soon
5. **Record any accrued expenses/income** for the period
6. **Record depreciation** for the period on any fixed assets
7. **Review the Balance Sheet's isBalanced check** — confirm Assets = Liabilities + Equity holds exactly
8. **Scan the P&L for anything unusual** — an expense category unexpectedly large or small
9. **Back up / export the data** for the period, especially before making any corrections

Only after this checklist is complete should a business owner truly trust that month's reports as an accurate reflection of what happened.
