## Appendix F: Accounting and Bookkeeping Glossary

An alphabetical reference of accounting/bookkeeping terms used throughout Appendices B-E, G-H.

---

**Accounts Payable (AP)** — Money the business owes to vendors/suppliers for goods or services already received but not yet paid for. A Liability. Normally exactly one AP account per business.

**Accounts Receivable (AR)** — Money owed TO the business by customers for goods/services already delivered but not yet paid for. An Asset. Normally exactly one AR account per business.

**Accrual Accounting** — Recording income when earned and expenses when incurred, regardless of when cash actually changes hands. The standard method for any business beyond the simplest cash-only operation. Contrast with Cash Basis Accounting.

**Accumulated Depreciation** — A contra-asset account that tracks the total depreciation recorded against a fixed asset over its life, reducing that asset's book value on the Balance Sheet.

**Asset** — Something a business owns or is owed that has value (cash, equipment, receivables). One of the five account types. Debit-normal.

**Balance Sheet** — A financial statement showing what a business owns (Assets), owes (Liabilities), and the owner's stake (Equity) as of one specific date — a snapshot, not a period.

**Bookkeeping** — The day-to-day recording of financial transactions. A subset of the broader discipline of accounting.

**Chart of Accounts (CoA)** — The complete, organized list of every account a business uses to categorize its money.

**Closing the Books** — The formal, periodic (usually annual) process of moving a period's Net Income into Retained Earnings and resetting Income/Expense accounts to zero for the new period.

**COGS (Cost of Goods Sold)** — The direct costs of producing what a business sells (materials, direct labor). A special Expense subtype, tracked separately to compute Gross Profit.

**Contra-account** — An account that reduces the balance of a related account rather than adding to it (e.g., Accumulated Depreciation reduces Fixed Assets; Owner's Draws reduces Equity).

**Credit** — One of the two "sides" in double-entry bookkeeping. Increases Liabilities, Equity, and Income; decreases Assets and Expenses. Not inherently "good" or "bad" — just a side.

**Current Asset** — An asset expected to be converted to cash or used within one year. Contrast with Fixed Asset.

**Current Liability** — A liability expected to be paid within one year. Contrast with Long-Term Liability.

**Debit** — The other of the two "sides" in double-entry bookkeeping. Increases Assets and Expenses; decreases Liabilities, Equity, and Income.

**Depreciation** — The systematic process of spreading a fixed asset's cost over its useful life, rather than expensing it all at once when purchased.

**Double-Entry Bookkeeping** — The system where every transaction affects at least two accounts, and total debits always equal total credits. The foundation of all real accounting systems.

**Equity** — The owner's stake in the business; what's left over after subtracting Liabilities from Assets. Credit-normal.

**Expense** — Money spent running the business. One of the five account types. Debit-normal.

**Fixed Asset** — An asset expected to provide value over multiple years (equipment, vehicles, property), typically depreciated over time.

**General Ledger** — The complete, chronological record of every journal line ever posted, organized by account.

**Gross Profit** — Total Income minus Cost of Goods Sold. Shows profitability of the core product/service before overhead expenses.

**Income** (also called Revenue) — Money earned from selling goods or services. Credit-normal.

**Income Statement** — Another name for the Profit & Loss report.

**Journal Entry** — A single recorded transaction, consisting of two or more lines (debits and credits) that must balance.

**Journal Line** — One row within a journal entry: a specific account plus a debit or credit amount.

**Liability** — Something a business owes to others. Credit-normal.

**Matching Principle** — The accounting concept that expenses should be recorded in the same period as the income they helped generate, even if cash for either hasn't moved yet.

**Materiality** — The accounting concept that very small, insignificant amounts don't need the full formal treatment that larger amounts do.

**Net Income / Net Profit** — Total Income minus Total Expenses (including COGS) for a period. "The bottom line."

**Net Loss** — The result when Total Expenses exceed Total Income for a period — a negative Net Income.

**Normal Balance** — Whether an account type naturally increases with a debit or a credit.

**Posting** — The act of formally saving a journal entry as final/real in the ledger.

**Prepaid Expense** — Money paid in advance for something not yet consumed, recorded as an Asset and gradually converted to an Expense as it's used.

**Profit & Loss (P&L)** — A financial statement showing Income minus Expenses over a period of time. Also called an Income Statement.

**Reconciliation** — The process of comparing a business's recorded transactions against an external source of truth (typically a bank statement) to confirm they match.

**Retained Earnings** — An Equity account holding accumulated Net Income from prior periods that hasn't been distributed to the owner(s).

**Reversal / Reversing Entry** — A journal entry that exactly cancels out a previous entry, used to correct mistakes without deleting historical records.

**Sales Tax Payable** — A Liability account holding sales tax collected from customers that must be remitted to a government tax authority.

**Single-Entry Bookkeeping** — A simpler system (like a basic checkbook register) that only tracks one side of each transaction. Breaks down for any business with credit sales/purchases, loans, or a need for a real Balance Sheet.

**Subtype** — A more specific classification within one of the five account types (e.g., "bank" or "accounts_receivable" within Asset).

**Unearned Revenue** (also called Deferred Revenue or Customer Deposits) — A Liability representing money received from a customer before the related goods/services have been delivered.

**Voiding** — Marking a transaction as cancelled/invalid without deleting its historical record, typically paired with a reversing entry.
