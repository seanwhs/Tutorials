# Comprehensive User Guide: The Complete Bookkeeping Cycle
## Part 6 of 7: Interpreting Results, Closing the Month, and Starting the Next Cycle

Continuing directly from Part 5. Riverside Design Studio's January financial statements are complete and balanced. Now Maya does what any business owner should do with these numbers: actually read them, ask questions of them, and decide what (if anything) to do differently going forward — before the cycle repeats for February.

---

### Step 1: Reading the P&L like an owner, not just a bookkeeper

Recall January's Profit & Loss showed $6,420 in net income, $993 in expenses, and $5,427 in Net Profit. A few questions Maya should ask (echoing Appendix E, section 2's guidance):

**Is this margin healthy?** Net Margin = Net Profit / Net Income = $5,427 / $6,420 ≈ 85%. For a service business with very low overhead (no inventory, no large physical footprint, one contractor expense), an 85% margin is genuinely strong — though it's worth remembering January didn't include Maya paying herself a "salary" in the traditional sense (her $2,000 draw isn't an expense at all, per Part 3) so this 85% overstates what a fully-loaded cost structure would look like if she ever hires real employees.

**Is any expense category surprisingly large or small?** Nothing jumps out as unusual for a first month — Contractor Expense ($400) is the single largest line, tied directly to the one large Northgate project, which is expected and healthy (the expense scales with the revenue-generating work, not as fixed overhead).

**Which revenue stream is doing more work?** Design Income ($5,850) dwarfs Consulting/retainer Income ($600) this month — largely because January included two large one-off projects (Bloom & Co, Northgate). Maya notes that as more retainer clients like Petal & Pine come on board, she'd expect Consulting Income's share to grow over time, giving her more predictable month-to-month revenue rather than relying on lumpy one-off projects.

### Step 2: Reading the Balance Sheet like an owner

Recall the Balance Sheet showed $21,002 in Total Assets, $575 in Total Liabilities, $20,427 in Total Equity.

**Is Riverside dangerously reliant on debt?** Total Liabilities ($575) versus Total Equity ($20,427) is an extremely conservative ratio — the business owes almost nothing relative to what it owns. This is typical (and healthy) for a very early-stage service business with no equipment financing or business loans yet.

**Current Ratio check (Appendix E, section 7):** Current Assets (Checking $14,558 + AR $4,500 = $19,058) divided by Current Liabilities ($575) ≈ 33. An extremely high current ratio — Riverside has far more short-term liquidity than it needs to cover its near-term obligations. This isn't a red flag (a ratio comfortably above 1 is reassuring), though extremely high ratios sometimes prompt a business owner to ask "should some of this cash be working harder" (e.g., invested, or reinvested into growing the business) — a judgment call outside the scope of bookkeeping itself.

**Is Accounts Receivable growing in a concerning way?** With only one client (Northgate) currently owing money, and it not yet overdue, there's no concern here yet — but Maya makes a mental note that if AR balances start growing faster than sales over future months, that would be worth investigating (a warning sign covered in Appendix E, section 3).

### Step 3: A word on what Maya does NOT need to do this month

It's worth being explicit about this, because beginners sometimes over-apply concepts they've just learned: Maya does NOT need to run a formal "closing the books" process (moving Net Income into Retained Earnings, zeroing out Income/Expense accounts) at the end of January. That formal closing happens once per FISCAL YEAR, not monthly (Appendix E, section 4; Appendix F glossary). Throughout the rest of the fiscal year, her Balance Sheet will keep showing a running, ever-updating "Net Income (current period, unclosed)" line that simply reflects the cumulative profit from the start of the fiscal year through whatever date she's checking — exactly as demonstrated in Part 5. Only at fiscal year-end does that accumulated amount get formally swept into Retained Earnings and the Income/Expense accounts reset to zero for the new year.

This means what Maya actually did in Part 4 (bank reconciliation, catching the missing fee, recording depreciation) IS the real, correct monthly "closing" process for a business at this stage — not a formal year-end closing, just the routine hygiene that makes a given month's numbers trustworthy before moving on.

### Step 4: Deciding on changes for February

Based on January's results, Maya considers a few adjustments going into February:
- Her early-payment discount (5% off, used once with Petal & Pine) cost her $30 this month. She decides to keep offering it — the faster cash collection is worth more to her than that small discount, especially now that she's tracking Sales Discounts explicitly and can watch this cost over time rather than it being invisible.
- She wants to onboard one more retainer client in February to grow her more predictable Consulting Income relative to lumpier one-off Design Income.
- She'll keep watching the AR aging report weekly (not just at month-end) once Northgate's invoice approaches its February 7th due date, so she can follow up promptly if it becomes overdue.

None of these are bookkeeping decisions per se — they're business decisions INFORMED by bookkeeping, which is really the entire point of maintaining real books in the first place (Appendix B, section 1's original framing: accounting exists to make business questions answerable with real numbers, not guesses).

### Step 5: Opening February — the cycle repeats

With January fully closed out, February begins exactly the way January did (Part 1 of this guide), except there's no need to record opening equity investments again — Riverside's Balance Sheet as of February 1st simply carries forward from where January 31st left off:

- Checking Account starts February at $14,558 (not $15,000 — it now reflects everything that happened)
- Accounts Receivable starts at $4,500 (Northgate's invoice still open)
- Accounts Payable starts at $455 (Adobe and Jordan still owed)
- Owner's Equity, Owner's Draws, and all Income/Expense accounts continue accumulating for the current fiscal year (they do NOT reset monthly — only at formal fiscal year-end, per Step 3 above)

February's activity would include: Northgate Dental hopefully paying their $4,500 invoice, Maya paying off Adobe and Jordan's bills before their due dates, February's Petal & Pine retainer invoice generating automatically, a new depreciation entry for the laptop, a new bank reconciliation at month-end, and so on — the exact same rhythm demonstrated across Parts 2 through 5 of this guide, applied to a new set of specific transactions.

This rhythm — record transactions as they happen, reconcile and adjust at month-end, generate and read the reports, make informed decisions, then repeat — IS the complete bookkeeping cycle. Every real business, from Riverside Design Studio to an actual QuickBooks-using enterprise, runs through some version of exactly this loop, month after month, year after year.
