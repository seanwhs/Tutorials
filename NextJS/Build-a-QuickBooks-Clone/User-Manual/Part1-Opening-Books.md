# Comprehensive User Guide: The Complete Bookkeeping Cycle
## Part 1 of 7: Meet the Company and Opening the Books

This guide walks through one full accounting cycle — from opening a brand new company's books, through a month of realistic transactions, to closing the month and producing trustworthy financial statements — using a single fictitious small business as a running example throughout. It's meant to complement the conceptual Appendices B through H (which explain individual concepts in isolation) by showing everything working together, in order, the way a real bookkeeper actually experiences it.

This guide is written for someone using the QuickBooks-clone application built across the tutorial series, but every concept applies equally to real QuickBooks, Xero, or manual bookkeeping — the underlying accounting is identical regardless of software.

---

### Meet the company: Riverside Design Studio

Throughout this guide we'll follow **Riverside Design Studio**, a small graphic design and branding consultancy founded by a sole owner, **Maya Chen**. Maya does branding, logo design, and marketing collateral for small businesses. She's just decided to formalize her freelance work into a real company with its own bank account and proper books, starting January 1st.

We picked a service business (not a retailer) deliberately — it keeps inventory/COGS complexity out of the main walkthrough (that's covered separately in Appendix D), letting us focus cleanly on the full cycle: opening balances, day-to-day transactions, month-end adjustments, financial statements, and closing.

### Step 1: Setting up the company and Chart of Accounts

Before any transaction can be recorded, Maya needs a Chart of Accounts (Appendix C covers this in full — here we apply it directly). She sets up her company in the app, which auto-seeds a sensible starter set of accounts. She reviews and slightly customizes them for her specific business:

```
1000 Checking Account (Asset, bank)
1100 Accounts Receivable (Asset, accounts_receivable)
1200 Undeposited Funds (Asset, other_current_asset)
1500 Computer Equipment (Asset, fixed_asset)

2000 Accounts Payable (Liability, accounts_payable)
2100 Business Credit Card (Liability, credit_card)

3000 Owner's Equity (Equity)
3100 Owner's Draws (Equity)
3200 Retained Earnings (Equity)

4000 Design Income (Income)
4100 Consulting Income (Income)

5100 Software Subscriptions Expense (Expense)
5200 Office Supplies Expense (Expense)
5300 Rent Expense (Expense)
5400 Utilities Expense (Expense)
5500 Contractor Expense (Expense)
5600 Advertising Expense (Expense)
5700 Bank Fees Expense (Expense)
```

Notice: no inventory, no COGS account — Riverside sells design SERVICES, not physical products, so there's nothing to hold in inventory. This is a completely valid, common Chart of Accounts shape for a service business (contrast this with a retail business's chart, which would need Inventory and Cost of Goods Sold accounts — see Appendix C, section 6).

### Step 2: Recording the opening investment

On January 1st, Maya deposits $15,000 of her personal savings into the new business checking account to get things started, and also contributes a laptop she already owned (fair market value $2,000) that she'll now use exclusively for business work.

**Journal Entry 1 — Owner's initial cash investment:**

| Date | Account | Debit | Credit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Jan 1 | Checking Account (1000) | $15,000 | |
| Jan 1 | Owner's Equity (3000) | | $15,000 |

**Journal Entry 2 — Owner contributes equipment:**

| Date | Account | Debit | Credit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Jan 1 | Computer Equipment (1500) | $2,000 | |
| Jan 1 | Owner's Equity (3000) | | $2,000 |

Two separate entries because they're two separate events, even though they happened the same day. Notice both entries increase an Asset (debit) and increase Equity (credit) — exactly the pattern from Appendix B's very first worked example, and Appendix D's examples 26-28.

### Step 3: Confirming the opening Balance Sheet balances

Before doing anything else, it's worth pausing to check the accounting equation holds after these two opening entries — this is a habit worth building from day one, not just at month-end.

```
Assets:
  Checking Account         $15,000
  Computer Equipment        $2,000
  Total Assets                          $17,000

Liabilities:
  Total Liabilities                          $0

Equity:
  Owner's Equity           $17,000
  Total Equity                          $17,000

Total Liabilities + Equity            $17,000
```

Assets ($17,000) = Liabilities + Equity ($0 + $17,000). Balanced, as expected — Riverside Design Studio's books are open and ready for real business activity.

### Step 4: Setting up customers and vendors

Before Maya can invoice anyone or receive a bill, she needs the actual businesses she works with set up in the system. She adds:

**Customers:**
- Bloom & Co (a florist, needs a new logo and business cards)
- Northgate Dental (needs a full rebrand: logo, signage design, website mockups)
- Petal & Pine Events (an ongoing monthly retainer client for social media graphics)

**Vendors:**
- Adobe (design software subscription)
- WeWork Riverside (monthly co-working desk rental)
- City Power & Light (utilities, if applicable to her workspace)
- Jordan Reyes (a freelance illustrator she occasionally subcontracts work to)

With the Chart of Accounts built, opening balances entered and balanced, and customers/vendors on file, Riverside Design Studio is ready for its first real month of business.
