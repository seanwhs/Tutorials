# Comprehensive User Guide: The Complete Bookkeeping Cycle
## Part 2 of 7: Weeks 1-2 — Sales, Invoicing, and Customer Payments

Continuing directly from Part 1. Riverside Design Studio's books are open with a $17,000 opening balance ($15,000 cash + $2,000 equipment, all Owner's Equity). Now the real business activity begins.

---

### January 3rd: A quick cash job for a walk-in client

A local bakery owner Maya knows personally asks for a simple flyer design, pays $150 cash on the spot after Maya delivers the final file the same day. No invoice was ever created — this was agreed and completed in one sitting.

**Journal Entry 3:**

| Date | Account | Debit | Credit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Jan 3 | Checking Account (1000) | $150 | |
| Jan 3 | Design Income (4000) | | $150 |

This matches Appendix D's example 1 exactly (cash sale, no invoice, no Accounts Receivable involved) — money and value changed hands at the same instant.

### January 5th: Invoice sent to Bloom & Co

Maya finishes the logo and business card design for Bloom & Co and sends an invoice for $1,200, due in 30 days (net 30 terms).

**Journal Entry 4:**

| Date | Account | Debit | Credit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Jan 5 | Accounts Receivable (1100) | $1,200 | |
| Jan 5 | Design Income (4000) | | $1,200 |

This is recorded the moment the invoice is sent, not when Bloom & Co actually pays — accrual accounting (Appendix H) recognizes the income now, because the work is genuinely done and earned. Riverside is now owed $1,200 it wasn't owed before.

At this point, in the actual application, this invoice would also trigger a background job (Part 19 of the tutorial series) that emails Bloom & Co a copy of the invoice, without making Maya wait for that email to send.

### January 8th: Invoice sent to Northgate Dental (a bigger project)

The Northgate Dental rebrand is a larger engagement. Maya invoices $4,500 for the full package (logo, signage, website mockups), due in 30 days.

**Journal Entry 5:**

| Date | Account | Debit | Credit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Jan 8 | Accounts Receivable (1100) | $4,500 | |
| Jan 8 | Design Income (4000) | | $4,500 |

At this point, Riverside's Accounts Receivable balance (the running total of everyone who owes the business money) is $1,200 (Bloom & Co) + $4,500 (Northgate) = $5,700. This is exactly the number that would show up as the AR account's balance on a Balance Sheet if checked today, and it's exactly the total that the AR Aging report (Appendix E, section 5) would show broken down by specific invoice.

### January 10th: First retainer invoice for Petal & Pine Events

Petal & Pine Events is an ongoing monthly retainer client. Maya sets up a recurring invoice template for $600/month, generated automatically on the 10th of each month (this mirrors Part 20 of the tutorial series' recurring invoice generator). January's invoice is generated:

**Journal Entry 6:**

| Date | Account | Debit | Credit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Jan 10 | Accounts Receivable (1100) | $600 | |
| Jan 10 | Consulting Income (4100) | | $600 |

Notice this is booked to Consulting Income (4100), not Design Income (4000) — Maya categorizes ongoing retainer/social-media work separately from one-off design projects, so her Profit & Loss report (Part 5 of this guide) can show her the split between the two revenue streams.

Running Accounts Receivable balance after this entry: $5,700 + $600 = $6,300.

### January 15th: Bloom & Co pays their invoice in full

Bloom & Co pays the full $1,200 invoice from January 5th via bank transfer.

**Journal Entry 7:**

| Date | Account | Debit | Credit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Jan 15 | Checking Account (1000) | $1,200 | |
| Jan 15 | Accounts Receivable (1100) | | $1,200 |

Notice Design Income is NOT touched again — it was already recorded in Journal Entry 4 back on January 5th. This transaction is purely about cash arriving and replacing a receivable (Appendix D, example 3). Bloom & Co's invoice status changes from "sent" to "paid" in the system, and their invoice drops off the AR Aging report entirely (nothing outstanding left to age).

Running Accounts Receivable balance after this payment: $6,300 - $1,200 = $5,100 (Northgate's $4,500 + Petal & Pine's $600).

### January 18th: Petal & Pine pays with an early-payment discount

Maya offers a small early-payment incentive on retainer invoices: 5% off if paid within a week. Petal & Pine pays within the window, sending $570 instead of the full $600.

**Journal Entry 8:**

| Date | Account | Debit | Credit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Jan 18 | Checking Account (1000) | $570 | |
| Jan 18 | Sales Discounts (Contra-Income) | $30 | |
| Jan 18 | Accounts Receivable (1100) | | $600 |

This matches Appendix D's example 5 exactly. The $30 discount is tracked in its own account so Maya can see, over time, how much revenue she's giving up to this incentive program — rather than it silently shrinking her Consulting Income figure with no visibility into why.

Running Accounts Receivable balance: $5,100 - $600 = $4,500 (just Northgate Dental remaining, not yet due).

### Mid-month checkpoint: a quick sanity check

Two weeks in, before continuing to Part 3's expense-side transactions, it's worth doing a rough sanity check on the books so far (a smaller-scale version of the month-end checklist covered fully in Appendix G, section 8):

**Checking Account running balance:**
$15,000 (opening) + $150 (bakery) + $1,200 (Bloom & Co payment) + $570 (Petal & Pine payment) = **$16,920**

**Accounts Receivable running balance:**
$4,500 (Northgate Dental only remains outstanding)

**Income recorded so far this month:**
$150 (bakery) + $1,200 (Bloom & Co) + $4,500 (Northgate) + $600 (Petal & Pine, before discount) = $6,450 gross, minus $30 in Sales Discounts = **$6,420 net income recorded**

These numbers aren't a formal report yet (that's Part 5), but this kind of quick manual tally is exactly the habit real bookkeepers build — catching an error two weeks in is far easier than catching it after a full quarter has piled up on top of it.
