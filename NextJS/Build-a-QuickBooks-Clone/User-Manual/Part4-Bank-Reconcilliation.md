# Comprehensive User Guide: The Complete Bookkeeping Cycle
## Part 4 of 7: Bank Reconciliation and Month-End Adjusting Entries

Continuing directly from Part 3. It's now January 31st, and before trusting any report generated from January's books, Maya runs through the reconciliation and adjustment steps every real bookkeeper performs before calling a month "closed" (this directly applies the checklist from Appendix G, section 8, to Riverside's actual numbers).

---

### Step 1: Pulling the real bank statement

Maya logs into her actual business bank account and downloads January's statement. According to her own recorded transactions (Parts 2 and 3), her Checking Account should sit at **$14,570**. The real bank statement shows a slightly different ending balance: **$14,558**.

A $12 difference. Before panicking, this is exactly what bank reconciliation (Appendix F glossary entry, Appendix G section 7) exists to investigate systematically, rather than guessing.

### Step 2: Comparing line by line

Maya goes through the bank statement transaction by transaction, checking each one against her own books:

- Jan 3: $150 deposit (bakery cash sale) — matches Journal Entry 3 ✓
- Jan 15: $1,200 deposit (Bloom & Co payment) — matches Journal Entry 7 ✓
- Jan 18: $570 deposit (Petal & Pine payment) — matches Journal Entry 8 ✓
- Jan 22: $350 withdrawal (WeWork bill payment) — matches Journal Entry 11 ✓
- Jan 30: $2,000 withdrawal (owner's draw) — matches Journal Entry 15 ✓
- **Jan 31: $12 withdrawal labeled "Monthly Account Fee" — NOT in Maya's books at all**

Found it. The bank charges a small monthly account maintenance fee that Maya's books never recorded, because it's automatically deducted by the bank with no bill or invoice ever generated — nothing prompted her to record it manually.

### Step 3: Recording the missing bank fee

This is a genuinely common, completely normal reconciliation finding — not a mistake Maya made, just a transaction that had no natural trigger to get recorded until this reconciliation step surfaced it.

**Journal Entry 16:**

| Date | Account | Debit | Credit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Jan 31 | Bank Fees Expense (5700) | $12 | |
| Jan 31 | Checking Account (1000) | | $12 |

This matches Appendix D's example 33 exactly. After recording this, Maya's books show a Checking Account balance of $14,570 − $12 = **$14,558** — now matching the real bank statement exactly.

### Step 4: Confirming the reconciliation is complete

With every bank statement line now matched to a corresponding entry in the books (and the one gap explained and recorded), January's Checking Account is officially reconciled. This is worth restating because it's the single most valuable habit in this entire guide: the bank statement is an independent, external source of truth Maya doesn't control, so agreement between it and her books is real evidence her records are accurate — not just an assumption.

### Step 5: Reviewing Accounts Receivable aging before closing

Maya checks her AR Aging report (Appendix E, section 5) as of January 31st. Only one invoice remains open: Northgate Dental's $4,500, issued January 8th with a due date of February 7th (30-day terms) — meaning it's not yet overdue as of month-end, correctly landing in the "Current" bucket. No action needed here this month, but Maya makes a mental note to check again in early February in case it becomes overdue.

### Step 6: Reviewing Accounts Payable aging before closing

Checking her AP Aging report, two bills remain open: Adobe's $55 (due February 4th) and Jordan Reyes's $400 (due February 8th) — both still in the "Current" bucket, nothing overdue. Maya notes she should pay both before their due dates in early February.

### Step 7: Considering whether any other accrued items are missing

Following Appendix G section 4's guidance on accrued expenses/income, Maya reviews whether anything was clearly earned or incurred in January but has no document yet. She has no outstanding payroll (she has no employees, only the contractor already billed and recorded), no unbilled client work sitting finished-but-not-invoiced, and no other services received without a bill. Nothing further to accrue this month.

### Step 8: The complete list of January's adjusting/reconciliation entries

For clarity, here is everything recorded specifically during this month-end process (as opposed to Parts 2-3's regular day-to-day activity):

- Journal Entry 14 (Part 3): Depreciation — $56
- Journal Entry 16 (this part): Missing bank fee — $12

Both are genuinely common types of month-end adjustments: depreciation is a scheduled, recurring adjustment every month for as long as a fixed asset is in use; the missing bank fee is a reconciliation-driven catch, the kind that surfaces differently every month depending on what the bank happens to charge or what got missed.

### A note on what "closing the books" does NOT mean here

It's worth being precise: Riverside is now ready to trust its January numbers and generate financial statements (Part 5), but this is different from the formal annual "closing the books" process (Appendix F glossary, Appendix E section 4) where a full fiscal year's Net Income gets moved into Retained Earnings and Income/Expense accounts reset to zero. That formal closing happens once a year, typically at fiscal year-end — what Maya has done here is simply the standard monthly hygiene that any well-run set of books goes through before trusting a given month's reports, a distinction covered more in Part 6 of this guide.
