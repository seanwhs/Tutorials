# Slide 146 – The Role of the Tabletop Exercise Facilitator

## Learning Objectives

* Understand the responsibilities of the facilitator
* Differentiate facilitation from instruction and evaluation
* Establish the facilitator as a neutral guide

### Primary Responsibilities

* Guide discussions
* Maintain exercise pace
* Encourage participation
* Keep participants focused on objectives
* Introduce injects
* Manage conflict
* Clarify assumptions
* Ensure psychological safety

### Facilitator vs. Instructor

| Facilitator              | Instructor         |
| ------------------------ | ------------------ |
| Guides discussion        | Delivers knowledge |
| Asks questions           | Provides answers   |
| Encourages collaboration | Conducts lectures  |
| Remains neutral          | Directs learning   |

### Core Principles

* Neutrality
* Professionalism
* Objectivity
* Respect
* Adaptability
* Active listening

### Speaker Notes

The facilitator is responsible for creating an environment where participants can openly discuss decisions, explore options, and learn from the experience. The facilitator should resist the urge to solve problems for participants and instead encourage critical thinking.

# Slide 150 – Asking Effective Questions

## Learning Objectives

* Encourage discussion
* Promote critical thinking

### Open-Ended Questions

* What information do you need?
* Who should be involved?
* What are your priorities?
* What risks concern you most?
* What would happen next?

### Probing Questions

* Why was that decision made?
* What alternatives were considered?
* What assumptions support that conclusion?
* How would you communicate this?

### Avoid

* Leading questions
* Yes/No questions
* Questions with implied answers

### Speaker Notes

Well-crafted questions encourage analysis rather than simple responses. Facilitators should use open-ended questions to stimulate thoughtful discussion and uncover assumptions.

# Slide 161 – Why Exercise Evaluation Matters

## Learning Objectives

* Understand the purpose of evaluating tabletop exercises
* Recognize the link between evaluation and organizational resilience
* Differentiate evaluation from participant testing

### Definition

Exercise evaluation is the structured process of assessing how effectively participants, teams, and organizational processes achieved the objectives of the tabletop exercise.

### Goals of Evaluation

* Measure objective achievement
* Identify capability gaps
* Validate policies and procedures
* Improve organizational resilience
* Support continuous improvement
* Justify investment in cybersecurity programs

### Evaluation Principles

* Objective
* Evidence-based
* Fair
* Repeatable
* Action-oriented

### Speaker Notes

The goal of evaluation is **not** to determine whether individuals passed or failed. Instead, it assesses whether the organization's people, processes, technologies, and governance structures are capable of responding effectively to cyber incidents.

Observers should remain as unobtrusive as possible. Their role is to collect evidence that will later support the Hot Wash and After Action Report (AAR).

# Slide 176 – Introduction to the Hot Wash

## Learning Objectives

* Define the purpose of a Hot Wash
* Understand how it differs from the After Action Report (AAR)
* Capture immediate observations while they are fresh

### What Is a Hot Wash?

A **Hot Wash** is an informal, structured debrief conducted immediately after the tabletop exercise. It provides participants, facilitators, observers, and the White Cell with an opportunity to discuss:

* What happened
* What went well
* What challenges were encountered
* What improvements are needed
* Immediate lessons learned

### Benefits

* Captures fresh observations
* Encourages open discussion
* Validates evaluator notes
* Identifies quick wins
* Improves future exercises

### Speaker Notes

The Hot Wash should occur immediately after the exercise while memories are still fresh. It is not intended to assign blame but to encourage constructive reflection and identify opportunities for improvement before developing the formal After Action Report.

Why?

↓
