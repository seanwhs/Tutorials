# Module Overview

Cybersecurity Tabletop Exercises (TTXs) are not merely operational training events—they are an essential component of enterprise governance, regulatory compliance, and organizational resilience. Many cybersecurity standards, industry regulations, and governance frameworks either recommend or require organizations to regularly validate their incident response, business continuity, crisis management, and cyber resilience capabilities.

Organizations increasingly operate under complex legal and regulatory obligations, including breach notification laws, privacy regulations, sector-specific cybersecurity requirements, and board-level governance expectations. A well-designed tabletop exercise provides an opportunity to test these obligations in a safe environment before an actual cyber incident occurs.

This module explores how tabletop exercises support governance, legal preparedness, regulatory compliance, enterprise risk management, and board oversight while ensuring that organizations remain aligned with international cybersecurity frameworks and industry best practices.

# 1. Governance and Cyber Resilience

## What is Cybersecurity Governance?

Cybersecurity governance is the system of leadership, policies, decision-making, accountability, and oversight used to ensure that cybersecurity supports organizational objectives while managing cyber risk effectively.

Governance defines:

* Who makes decisions
* Who owns cyber risk
* How risks are monitored
* How resources are allocated
* How accountability is enforced
* How resilience is measured

### Why Governance Matters

Without effective governance:

* Incident response becomes inconsistent.
* Executive decisions are delayed.
* Accountability becomes unclear.
* Regulatory obligations may be missed.
* Business recovery is slowed.

---

# 2. Why Governance Should Be Exercised

Policies and procedures alone do not guarantee effective governance.

Tabletop exercises allow organizations to validate:

* Decision-making authority
* Escalation processes
* Executive communications
* Crisis leadership
* Regulatory reporting
* Cross-functional coordination
* Governance effectiveness

### Example

During a ransomware attack:

Questions include:

* Who declares a major incident?
* Who approves public statements?
* Who authorizes negotiations?
* Who notifies regulators?
* Who informs customers?

These decisions should be practiced before a real incident occurs.

# 12. Roles and Accountability

## RACI Matrix Example

| Activity         | CISO | CIO | CEO | Legal | SOC |
| ---------------- | ---- | --- | --- | ----- | --- |
| Detect Incident  | A    | I   | I   | I     | R   |
| Escalate         | A    | C   | I   | I     | R   |
| Public Statement | C    | C   | A   | R     | I   |
| Notify Regulator | C    | I   | A   | R     | I   |
| Recovery         | C    | A   | I   | I     | R   |

**R = Responsible**
**A = Accountable**
**C = Consulted**
**I = Informed**

# Practical Exercise

## Scenario

Your organization operates in the financial services sector and must comply with regulatory requirements while maintaining customer trust during a cyber crisis.

### Group Activity

Working in teams:

1. Design a governance structure for your organization's cybersecurity incident response program.
2. Develop a RACI matrix covering key cyber incident response activities.
3. Identify the regulatory frameworks and legal obligations applicable to your organization.
4. Define the board-level decisions that should be exercised during a ransomware tabletop exercise.
5. Select five governance KPIs that will be reported to executive leadership after the exercise.

### Deliverable
