# Part 2.1 — Rules of Engagement (RoE): Foundations and Design Principles

> *A tabletop exercise begins long before the first inject is delivered. It begins when every participant understands why they are there, what is expected of them, and the boundaries within which the exercise will operate.*

The **Rules of Engagement (RoE)** is the foundational governance document for a tabletop exercise. It establishes the purpose of the exercise, defines its operational boundaries, clarifies participant expectations, and creates the conditions for productive discussion and learning.

Unlike the exercise scenario, which tells the story of the simulated incident, the RoE defines **how the exercise itself will be conducted**. It is the contract between facilitators, participants, observers, and sponsors that ensures the exercise remains safe, realistic, and focused on improvement.

Every tabletop exercise—regardless of size or maturity—should begin with an approved RoE.

---

# What Is a Rules of Engagement Document?

A Rules of Engagement document is a formal statement describing the governance of the exercise.

It answers questions such as:

* Why are we conducting this exercise?
* What are we trying to achieve?
* What systems and processes are included?
* What topics are deliberately excluded?
* Who has authority to pause or stop the exercise?
* How should participants communicate?
* How will observations be recorded?
* How will findings be used after the exercise?

Without clear answers to these questions, even a well-designed scenario can become confusing, inconsistent, or counterproductive.

---

# Why Every Exercise Needs an RoE

It is tempting to view the RoE as administrative paperwork. In reality, it is one of the most important documents in the entire exercise package.

A well-written RoE provides five essential benefits.

## 1. Establishes Shared Expectations

Participants should never have to guess:

* what is expected of them,
* whether they are allowed to make assumptions,
* who makes decisions, or
* what success looks like.

The RoE aligns everyone before the exercise begins.

---

## 2. Protects Psychological Safety

One of the most common reasons tabletop exercises fail is that participants feel they are being evaluated rather than supported.

An effective RoE makes it clear that:

* the exercise is not a performance review,
* mistakes are expected,
* discussion is encouraged,
* questions are welcomed,
* findings improve processes rather than assign blame.

This creates an environment where participants are more willing to share uncertainty, challenge assumptions, and explore alternative approaches.

---

## 3. Prevents Scope Creep

During an engaging exercise, discussions naturally expand.

Participants may begin investigating systems that were never intended to be part of the scenario or debating policies unrelated to the exercise objectives.

The RoE establishes clear boundaries.

For example:

**In Scope**

* Incident response process
* Executive communications
* Vendor coordination

**Out of Scope**

* Procurement decisions
* Technology replacement projects
* Budget approval discussions
* Live production changes

Clearly defining scope helps facilitators redirect conversations without discouraging participation.

---

## 4. Defines Decision Authority

Exercises often require facilitator decisions that affect pacing or realism.

Examples include:

* delaying an inject,
* skipping an inject,
* pausing the exercise,
* introducing additional information,
* ending discussion,
* escalating to executive leadership.

The RoE documents who has authority to make these decisions, preventing confusion during the session.

---

## 5. Creates a Repeatable Standard

Organizations rarely conduct only one tabletop exercise.

Over time, multiple facilitators, business units, and scenarios should all operate under the same governance model.

The RoE becomes the standard against which future exercises are planned and executed.

# Core Design Principles

Every effective RoE should be built around a small number of enduring principles.

These principles remain valid regardless of organization, technology, or scenario.

---

## Learning Before Evaluation

The primary purpose of a tabletop exercise is to improve organizational capability.

Although participant performance may be observed, the objective is not to "pass" or "fail."

Instead, facilitators should ask:

* What did we learn?
* Which assumptions were challenged?
* Which processes worked well?
* Where can we improve?

Exercises should identify opportunities for improvement rather than assign grades.

---

## Realism Without Risk

Exercises should feel authentic.

However, realism should never compromise safety.

For example:

Acceptable:

* realistic SIEM alerts,
* simulated executive emails,
* fictional journalist inquiries,
* mock vendor communications.

Unacceptable:

* accessing production systems,
* changing firewall rules,
* disabling real accounts,
* sending simulated phishing emails to uninformed staff during a discussion-based exercise.

Realism should enhance discussion—not introduce operational risk.

---

## Transparency

Participants should understand:

* exercise objectives,
* ground rules,
* communication channels,
* facilitator roles,
* evaluation criteria.

Unexpected scenario events are appropriate.

Unexpected exercise rules are not.

Transparency builds trust between facilitators and participants.

---

## Consistency

The White Cell should provide consistent answers throughout the exercise.

If multiple facilitators respond differently to similar questions, participants quickly lose confidence in the scenario.

The RoE helps maintain consistency by documenting:

* assumptions,
* scenario truth,
* decision authority,
* approved responses.

---

## Respect for Participants

Every participant contributes expertise.

Facilitators should encourage discussion rather than dominate it.

Respect includes:

* allowing participants to finish explanations,
* acknowledging differing viewpoints,
* avoiding public criticism,
* recognizing operational constraints,
* encouraging quieter participants to contribute.

Psychological safety is created through facilitator behavior as much as written policy.

---

# The No-Blame Principle

Perhaps the single most important section of an RoE is the no-blame statement.

Participants should understand that:

* observations are recorded,
* findings relate to processes,
* recommendations improve capability,
* individuals are not singled out.

A typical statement might read:

> *This exercise is conducted solely for organizational learning and capability improvement. Observations and findings will focus on processes, communications, governance, and technical controls. Individual performance will not be evaluated unless explicitly stated in the exercise objectives.*

This statement should be communicated verbally during the opening briefing as well as documented in the RoE.

---

# Facilitator Responsibilities

The RoE places significant responsibility on facilitators.

Facilitators are expected to:

* maintain neutrality,
* manage exercise pacing,
* encourage balanced discussion,
* enforce agreed boundaries,
* ensure participant safety,
* preserve realism,
* capture observations objectively.

The facilitator is not an examiner or adversary.

Instead, the facilitator acts as a guide who enables productive discussion while keeping the exercise aligned with its objectives.

---

# Participant Responsibilities

Participants also have responsibilities.

They should:

* engage actively,
* make decisions based on available information,
* communicate assumptions openly,
* remain within exercise scope,
* respect differing perspectives,
* avoid introducing real operational changes,
* contribute honestly during debriefs.

Successful tabletop exercises depend on collaboration rather than competition.

---

# Observer Responsibilities

Observers occupy a unique role.

They should:

* remain silent unless instructed otherwise,
* record objective observations,
* avoid influencing participant decisions,
* capture notable quotes and assumptions,
* identify communication patterns,
* document decision timelines.

Observers should evaluate the exercise process—not the individuals participating.

---

# White Cell Responsibilities

The White Cell safeguards the integrity of the exercise.

Its responsibilities include:

* maintaining scenario truth,
* managing inject timing,
* answering clarification requests,
* introducing additional information,
* adjusting pacing when necessary,
* documenting deviations from the MSEL.

All significant scenario changes should be coordinated through the White Cell to preserve consistency.

---

# Governance Before Gameplay

A tabletop exercise is often compared to a strategic game.

The analogy is useful—but incomplete.

Unlike a game, a tabletop exercise has organizational consequences. Decisions made during discussion may influence future policies, investments, and operational procedures.

For this reason, governance must always precede gameplay.

The Rules of Engagement provides that governance by establishing clear expectations, protecting participants, and creating the structure within which meaningful learning can occur.

Every subsequent exercise artifact—from the scenario and MSEL to the After Action Review—builds upon the foundation established by the RoE.

The stronger that foundation, the more effective and repeatable the tabletop exercise program becomes.
