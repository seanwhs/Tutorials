# Part 7.2 — Production Facilitator Checklist & Operational Runbook

> *A successful tabletop exercise rarely depends on facilitator improvisation. It depends on disciplined preparation, consistent execution, and structured follow-up.*

The facilitator is responsible for creating an environment in which participants can learn, collaborate, and make realistic decisions. While the scenario and MSEL provide the exercise content, the facilitator ensures that the exercise proceeds safely, maintains momentum, and achieves its objectives.

This runbook is intended to be used before, during, and after every tabletop exercise.

---

# Facilitator Responsibilities

The Lead Facilitator is responsible for:

* Planning the exercise.
* Confirming objectives.
* Coordinating the White Cell.
* Maintaining psychological safety.
* Managing exercise pace.
* Delivering injects.
* Encouraging discussion.
* Protecting exercise scope.
* Conducting the Hot Wash.
* Initiating the After Action Review.

The facilitator guides the conversation but does **not** solve the scenario for participants.

# During the Exercise

The facilitator should continually monitor:

* pace,
* participation,
* engagement,
* discussion quality,
* exercise objectives,
* time.

Avoid dominating discussions.

Participants should perform most of the talking.

---

# Inject Management

Before releasing an inject, verify:

☐ Previous discussion has concluded.

☐ Critical decision has been documented.

☐ Observer notes are complete.

☐ Participants are ready.

Deliver injects naturally.

Avoid announcing:

> "Here comes the next inject."

Instead introduce information realistically.

Examples:

> "The SOC has just received another alert."

or

> "Your communications manager receives the following email."

---

# Managing Discussion

Encourage participants to explain:

* reasoning,
* assumptions,
* uncertainties,
* alternatives.

Useful facilitation questions include:

* What concerns you most?
* What information do you need?
* Who needs to be involved?
* What are your priorities?
* What could go wrong?
* What assumptions are you making?

Questions should stimulate discussion rather than direct it.

---

# Managing Time

If discussion becomes excessively detailed:

Summarize.

Confirm decisions.

Redirect.

Example:

> "That is an important technical issue. For today's exercise, let's assume that analysis can continue while we focus on organizational decision-making."

Maintain momentum without dismissing participant contributions.

---

# Managing Dominant Participants

If one participant dominates:

Thank them.

Invite additional perspectives.

Example:

> "Let's hear how Communications would approach this."

Balanced participation improves exercise outcomes.

---

# Supporting Quiet Participants

Invite quieter participants into the discussion.

Example:

> "Legal has not yet commented. Would you like to add anything?"

Avoid putting participants on the spot unnecessarily.

---

# Handling Unexpected Questions

Participants frequently ask:

* "Would this system detect that?"
* "Can we access those logs?"
* "Would Legal already know?"

Respond consistently.

If the answer is unknown:

Consult the White Cell.

Avoid inventing information.

---

# Decision Gates

Before advancing the scenario, verify that participants have discussed:

☐ Incident classification.

☐ Escalation.

☐ Executive notification.

☐ Communications.

☐ Containment.

☐ Recovery.

Do not advance simply because the clock says so.

---

# Managing Exercise Drift

Watch for:

* excessive technical detail,
* unrelated discussions,
* repeated debate,
* unrealistic assumptions.

Redirect politely.

Return discussion to the exercise objectives.

---

# White Cell Coordination

Maintain continuous communication.

Track:

* completed injects,
* timing adjustments,
* participant questions,
* contingency decisions,
* observer feedback.

A synchronized White Cell enables smooth facilitation.

---

# Exercise Closing

Before ending the scenario, confirm:

☐ Major objectives addressed.

☐ Critical discussions completed.

☐ Recovery discussed.

☐ Final decisions recorded.

Avoid ending abruptly.

Provide a natural transition into the Hot Wash.

---

# Hot Wash

Immediately after the exercise ask:

What worked well?

What surprised you?

What challenged you?

What assumptions changed?

What would you do differently?

Capture comments without debate.

The objective is gathering impressions while they remain fresh.

---

# Facilitator Debrief

Immediately after participants leave:

Meet with:

* White Cell,
* Observers,
* Exercise Director.

Discuss:

* pacing,
* inject effectiveness,
* participant engagement,
* scenario realism,
* improvements.

Document observations immediately.

---

# Common Facilitation Pitfalls

Avoid:

* Talking more than participants.
* Revealing hidden scenario information.
* Rushing discussions.
* Ignoring quieter participants.
* Solving participant problems.
* Defending the scenario.
* Treating the exercise as a technical exam.

Your role is to facilitate learning, not demonstrate expertise.

# Facilitator Guiding Principles

Keep these principles visible throughout every exercise:

* Facilitate rather than instruct.
* Ask more questions than you answer.
* Encourage collaboration over competition.
* Protect psychological safety.
* Focus on organizational capability.
* Adapt pacing to participant needs.
* Base discussions on evidence.
* Keep learning at the center of every decision.

A facilitator's success is measured not by how smoothly the exercise follows the script, but by how effectively participants engage with uncertainty, challenge assumptions, and identify opportunities to strengthen organizational resilience.
