# Appendix I.3.2 — Enterprise Cyber Risk Management Assessment Workbook

> *"Cyber risk management is not about eliminating risk. It is about making informed business decisions under uncertainty."*

---

# Purpose

Effective cyber resilience begins with understanding risk.

Organizations that recover well from cyber incidents generally have one characteristic in common:

**They understand which risks matter most to the business before a crisis occurs.**

This workbook evaluates whether cyber risk management is:

* Business-driven.
* Repeatable.
* Evidence-based.
* Integrated into enterprise governance.
* Continuously improved.

---

# Assessment Scope

This workbook evaluates the organization's capability across the complete cyber risk lifecycle.

The assessment covers:

* Risk governance.
* Risk identification.
* Risk analysis.
* Risk evaluation.
* Risk treatment.
* Risk monitoring.
* Risk reporting.
* Third-party risk integration.
* Emerging technology risk.
* Continuous improvement.

---

# Capability Objectives

A mature cyber risk management capability ensures that:

✓ Cyber risks are understood in business terms.

✓ Critical business services receive appropriate protection.

✓ Risk decisions are owned by the business.

✓ Investments are aligned to enterprise priorities.

✓ Risk is continuously reassessed as the threat landscape evolves.

# R1 — Risk Governance

## Objective

Determine whether cyber risk management is integrated into enterprise governance.

---

## Assessment Questions

Rate each statement from **0 (Not Implemented)** to **5 (Adaptive)**.

| #     | Assessment Question                                                 | Score |
| ----- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- | :---: |
| R1.1  | A formally approved cyber risk management framework exists.         |       |
| R1.2  | Cyber risk management aligns with enterprise risk management (ERM). |       |
| R1.3  | Risk ownership is formally assigned.                                |       |
| R1.4  | Risk appetite is documented and approved by executive leadership.   |       |
| R1.5  | Cyber risk is reviewed at defined governance forums.                |       |
| R1.6  | Business units participate in cyber risk decisions.                 |       |
| R1.7  | Risk acceptance requires documented approval.                       |       |
| R1.8  | Risk governance roles are regularly reviewed.                       |       |
| R1.9  | Cyber risk reporting supports executive decision-making.            |       |
| R1.10 | Governance processes are periodically audited.                      |       |

---

## Evidence Checklist

Examples include:

* Enterprise risk management framework.
* Risk committee charter.
* Risk appetite statement.
* Governance meeting minutes.
* Risk register.
* Internal audit reports.

---

## Interview Questions

* Who owns cyber risk?
* How is cyber risk escalated?
* What constitutes an unacceptable cyber risk?
* How frequently is the enterprise risk register reviewed?

---

## Common Weaknesses

Typical observations include:

* Risk owned exclusively by IT.
* No documented cyber risk appetite.
* Weak integration with enterprise risk management.
* Inconsistent executive participation.

# R3 — Asset & Business Service Criticality

Organizations often protect infrastructure rather than business outcomes.

This section evaluates whether:

* Critical services are identified.
* Business impact is understood.
* Recovery priorities are defined.
* Service dependencies are mapped.

---

### Evidence Examples

* Business Impact Analysis (BIA).
* Service catalog.
* Dependency maps.
* Recovery objectives (RTO/RPO).
* Critical asset inventory.

# R5 — Risk Analysis & Evaluation

Assess whether cyber risks are analyzed consistently.

Consider:

* Likelihood.
* Business impact.
* Financial consequences.
* Operational disruption.
* Safety implications.
* Regulatory exposure.
* Reputational damage.

Organizations should use a documented methodology to evaluate risk.

---

# R6 — Risk Treatment

Evaluate how identified risks are managed.

Risk treatment options include:

* Mitigate.
* Transfer.
* Accept.
* Avoid.

Assessment should verify that treatment decisions are:

* Documented.
* Approved.
* Tracked.
* Reviewed.

---

# R7 — Risk Monitoring & Reporting

Risk management is continuous.

Evaluate:

* Risk dashboards.
* Executive reporting.
* Trend analysis.
* Key Risk Indicators (KRIs).
* Risk tolerance monitoring.
* Escalation triggers.

---

# R8 — Third-Party & Supply Chain Risk

Assess whether external dependencies are effectively governed.

Consider:

* Supplier classification.
* Due diligence.
* Security assessments.
* Contractual requirements.
* Continuous monitoring.
* Exit planning.

---

### Evidence Examples

* Vendor risk assessments.
* Supplier inventories.
* Security questionnaires.
* Contract clauses.
* Independent assurance reports.

---

# R9 — Emerging Technology Risk

Evaluate how the organization assesses risks associated with:

* Artificial intelligence.
* Machine learning.
* Generative AI.
* Internet of Things.
* Quantum computing.
* Cloud-native architectures.
* Low-code/no-code platforms.

The assessment should determine whether innovation is accompanied by proportionate risk evaluation.

---

# R10 — Continuous Risk Improvement

Determine whether risk management evolves over time.

Assessment areas include:

* Lessons learned from incidents.
* Exercise findings.
* Internal audits.
* External assessments.
* Regulatory changes.
* Threat landscape evolution.
