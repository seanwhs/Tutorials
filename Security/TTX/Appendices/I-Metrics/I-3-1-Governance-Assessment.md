# Appendix I.3.1 — Governance & Leadership Assessment Workbook

> *"Technology does not create cyber resilience. Leadership does."*

---

# Purpose

Governance is the foundation of enterprise cyber resilience.

Organizations rarely fail because they lack security technologies. They fail because:

* Accountability is unclear.
* Decision-making is delayed.
* Business priorities are disconnected from cybersecurity.
* Risk ownership is poorly defined.
* Investments are reactive rather than strategic.

This workbook assesses whether governance enables the organization to anticipate, withstand, recover from, and adapt to cyber disruption.

---

# Assessment Scope

This domain evaluates:

* Executive governance.
* Board oversight.
* Strategic alignment.
* Risk ownership.
* Decision-making.
* Policy framework.
* Funding and investment.
* Accountability.
* Continuous improvement.

---

# Capability Objectives

A mature governance capability ensures that:

✓ Cyber resilience is treated as an enterprise business issue.

✓ Executive leadership understands cyber risk.

✓ Decision ownership is clearly defined.

✓ Business priorities drive cybersecurity investments.

✓ Governance continues during crises.

# G1 — Executive Governance

## Objective

Determine whether executive leadership actively governs cyber resilience.

---

## Assessment Questions

Rate each statement from **0 (Not Implemented)** to **5 (Adaptive)**.

| #    | Assessment Question                                                     | Score |
| ---- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- | :---: |
| G1.1 | Executive leadership has formally approved a cyber resilience strategy. |       |
| G1.2 | Cyber resilience objectives align with business strategy.               |       |
| G1.3 | Executive leadership regularly reviews cyber resilience performance.    |       |
| G1.4 | Business leaders actively participate in cyber resilience planning.     |       |
| G1.5 | Cyber resilience is included in strategic planning discussions.         |       |

---

## Evidence Checklist

Evidence may include:

* Executive committee minutes.
* Strategy documents.
* Annual business plans.
* Governance charters.
* Steering committee records.

---

## Interview Questions

Examples:

* Who owns enterprise cyber resilience?
* How often is cyber resilience discussed by the executive team?
* How are strategic priorities reflected in cybersecurity investments?

---

## Common Weaknesses

Typical findings include:

* Cybersecurity treated as an IT issue.
* Limited executive engagement.
* Strategy disconnected from business priorities.

---

## Improvement Actions

Recommended actions:

* Establish an executive cyber resilience steering committee.
* Include cyber resilience in enterprise strategy reviews.
* Define measurable executive objectives.

# G2 — Board Oversight

## Objective

Assess whether the Board provides effective oversight of cyber resilience.

---

## Assessment Questions

| #    | Assessment Question                                                | Score |
| ---- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ | :---: |
| G2.1 | The Board receives regular cyber resilience reports.               |       |
| G2.2 | Reporting focuses on business risk rather than technical activity. |       |
| G2.3 | The Board reviews significant cyber exercises.                     |       |
| G2.4 | The Board approves cyber risk appetite.                            |       |
| G2.5 | Cyber resilience is integrated into enterprise risk oversight.     |       |

---

## Evidence Checklist

Examples:

* Board agendas.
* Risk committee minutes.
* Executive dashboards.
* Annual cyber reviews.

---

## Maturity Indicators

### Level 1

Board engagement occurs only after significant incidents.

### Level 3

Cyber resilience is reviewed routinely as part of enterprise risk management.

### Level 5

The Board actively challenges assumptions, monitors resilience trends, and influences long-term capability investments.

# G3 — Cyber Strategy

## Assessment Focus

Evaluate whether cybersecurity strategy supports business resilience.

Example assessment areas:

* Strategic objectives.
* Multi-year roadmap.
* Alignment with digital transformation.
* Emerging technology planning.
* Resilience priorities.

# G4 — Risk Governance

Evaluate:

* Risk ownership.
* Risk acceptance.
* Risk appetite.
* Enterprise integration.
* Escalation processes.

---

# G5 — Decision Authorities

Assess whether authority is clearly defined for:

* Incident declaration.
* Crisis activation.
* Business shutdown.
* Recovery approval.
* Regulatory notification.
* Public communication.
* Risk acceptance.

A RACI matrix should support these decisions.

---

# G6 — Policy Framework

Evaluate:

* Policy hierarchy.
* Standards.
* Procedures.
* Governance documentation.
* Review frequency.

Policies should be understandable, maintained, and aligned with business objectives.

---

# G7 — Investment & Funding

Assess whether cyber resilience investments are:

* Risk-based.
* Business-aligned.
* Prioritized.
* Measured for effectiveness.

Questions include:

* Is funding linked to strategic objectives?
* Are investments evaluated against resilience outcomes?
* Are multi-year funding plans established?

---

# G8 — Crisis Leadership

Evaluate executive preparedness for major cyber events.

Consider:

* Executive tabletop participation.
* Crisis decision-making.
* Communication.
* Delegation of authority.
* Business continuity leadership.

---

# G9 — Continuous Improvement

Determine whether governance evolves based on operational experience.

Assessment areas include:

* Lessons learned integration.
* Audit findings.
* Exercise improvements.
* Maturity reassessments.
* Strategic reviews.

# Interpretation Guide

|   Score | Maturity Level | Interpretation                                                                            |
| ------: | -------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 0.0–0.9 | Initial        | Governance is largely absent or reactive.                                                 |
| 1.0–1.9 | Developing     | Basic governance structures exist but are inconsistent.                                   |
| 2.0–2.9 | Defined        | Governance processes are documented and generally followed.                               |
| 3.0–3.9 | Managed        | Governance is measured, integrated, and consistently applied.                             |
| 4.0–5.0 | Adaptive       | Governance continuously evolves based on business strategy, metrics, and lessons learned. |
