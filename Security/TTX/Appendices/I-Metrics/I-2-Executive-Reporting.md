# Appendix I.2 — Board and Executive Cyber Resilience Reporting Templates

> *"Executives do not need more cybersecurity data. They need better cybersecurity decisions."*

---

# I.2 Board and Executive Reporting Framework

## Purpose

Cyber resilience reporting exists to support governance, not operational monitoring.

A well-designed executive report should answer five questions:

1. **What is our current cyber risk position?**
2. **Has our resilience improved since the last reporting period?**
3. **Where are our greatest business risks?**
4. **What decisions require executive attention?**
5. **Are we investing in the right capabilities?**

Board reporting should avoid excessive technical detail and instead communicate risk in business terms.

---

# Executive Reporting Principles

Every report should be:

* Concise
* Business-focused
* Trend-oriented
* Actionable
* Decision-driven

Avoid reporting technical metrics without explaining their business implications.

---

# Executive Summary

Example:

> During the reporting period, no material cyber incidents affected business operations. Recovery capability improved following two enterprise exercises, although third-party cyber risk remains above the Board's target tolerance. Executive approval is requested for additional investment in supplier assurance and identity governance.

Cyber risks should be presented using a business-oriented risk matrix.

| Risk                      | Likelihood | Business Impact | Trend | Risk Owner  |
| ------------------------- | ---------- | --------------- | ----- | ----------- |
| Ransomware                | High       | Critical        | ↑     | CISO        |
| Cloud Identity Compromise | Medium     | High            | →     | CIO         |

# I.2.8 Executive Decision Register

Capture significant governance decisions.

| Date | Decision                                | Owner | Status      |
| ---- | --------------------------------------- | ----- | ----------- |

Maintaining a decision register provides traceability for Board oversight.

---

# I.2.9 Quarterly CISO Report Structure

A recommended quarterly report includes:

1. Executive Summary
2. Current Threat Landscape
3. Business Risk Assessment
4. Major Incidents
5. Resilience Metrics
6. Exercise Outcomes
7. Regulatory Updates
8. Third-Party Risk
9. Investment Progress
10. Decisions Requested

The report should generally be limited to 10–15 pages, with detailed technical information available in appendices.

---

# I.2.10 Annual Board Cyber Review

At least annually, the Board should receive a comprehensive review covering:

* Changes in the threat landscape.
* Progress against the cyber strategy.
* Results of major exercises.
* Independent assessments and audits.
* Regulatory developments.
* Emerging technology risks (including AI and quantum computing).
* Strategic investment priorities for the coming year.

The review should support long-term planning rather than operational oversight.

---

# Key Success Indicators

Executive reporting is effective when Board members can confidently answer:

* What are our most significant cyber risks?
* Are we becoming more resilient?
* Where are we below our target capability?
* What decisions require our attention?
* How do our investments reduce business risk?

If the Board cannot answer these questions, the reporting process should be redesigned.
