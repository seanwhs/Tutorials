# Appendix E — Cybersecurity Tabletop Exercise Glossary

> *A common vocabulary is essential for effective incident response. Teams cannot coordinate efficiently if different participants assign different meanings to the same terms. This glossary establishes a shared language for planning, conducting, evaluating, and improving cybersecurity tabletop exercises.*

---

# How to Use This Glossary

This glossary serves three purposes:

1. **Reference** — A quick lookup for unfamiliar terms encountered throughout this handbook.
2. **Consistency** — Standardized definitions that reduce ambiguity during planning and facilitation.
3. **Learning** — A foundation for participants who are new to cybersecurity exercises or incident response.

Where applicable, terms align with commonly used concepts from:

* NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)
* NIST SP 800-61 (Incident Response)
* NIST SP 800-84 (Guide to Test, Training, and Exercise Programs)
* MITRE ATT&CK
* CISA Cybersecurity Exercise guidance
* ISO/IEC 27035 (Incident Management)
* ISO 22301 (Business Continuity Management)

This glossary is intended as a practical guide rather than a formal standards document.

---

# A

## After Action Review (AAR)

A structured evaluation conducted after an exercise or incident to identify observations, findings, recommendations, and improvement actions.

**See also:** Hot Wash, Improvement Roadmap

---

## Asset

Any information, application, service, infrastructure component, device, or business capability that has value to the organization.

---

## Assumption

A condition accepted as true for the purpose of an exercise. Assumptions simplify scenarios and establish boundaries but should always be documented.

---

## Attack Path

The sequence of actions an adversary performs to achieve an objective, such as credential theft, privilege escalation, lateral movement, and data exfiltration.

---

## ATT&CK

MITRE ATT&CK is a publicly available knowledge base describing adversary tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs). It is widely used to build realistic exercise scenarios and map participant responses.

---

# B

## Backup Integrity

The confidence that backup data is complete, accurate, uncompromised, and recoverable.

---

## Blue Team

The defenders responsible for protecting systems, detecting threats, and responding to security incidents.

---

## Business Continuity

The capability of an organization to continue delivering critical products and services during and after a disruptive event.

---

## Business Impact Analysis (BIA)

A structured assessment used to identify critical business processes, dependencies, acceptable downtime, and recovery priorities.

---

# C

## Chain of Custody

The documented process used to preserve and track evidence throughout an investigation to maintain its integrity.

---

## Communications Plan

A documented approach describing how information will be shared during an exercise or incident, including audiences, channels, approval processes, and timing.

---

## Compromise

The successful violation of a system's confidentiality, integrity, or availability.

---

## Containment

Actions taken to limit an incident's spread or impact while preserving evidence for investigation.

---

## Critical Asset

An asset whose loss or disruption would significantly affect organizational operations.

---

## Cyber Resilience

The ability of an organization to anticipate, withstand, recover from, and adapt to adverse cyber events.

---

# D

## Decision Point

A planned moment during an exercise where participants must choose between multiple response options.

---

## Detection

The identification of suspicious or malicious activity through monitoring, analytics, user reports, or other evidence.

---

## Digital Forensics

The process of collecting, preserving, analyzing, and presenting digital evidence to understand an incident.

---

# E

## Escalation

The formal process of increasing the visibility, priority, or authority assigned to an incident.

---

## Exercise Control (White Cell)

The group responsible for managing scenario progression, inject timing, participant questions, and exercise consistency.

---

## Exercise Objective

A measurable capability or behavior that the exercise is designed to evaluate.

---

## Exercise Scope

The organizational boundaries, systems, participants, and assumptions included in the exercise.

---

# F

## Facilitator

The individual responsible for guiding discussion, maintaining exercise pace, encouraging participation, and ensuring objectives are achieved.

---

## Finding

A validated conclusion drawn from observations gathered during an exercise.

Findings should always be evidence-based.

---

## Full-Scale Exercise

A highly realistic exercise involving operational teams, systems, communications, and external coordination. It represents the highest maturity level in most exercise programs.

---

# G

## Gap

A weakness or deficiency identified during planning, execution, or evaluation that requires improvement.

---

## Governance

The leadership, oversight, decision-making, and accountability structures supporting cybersecurity and incident response.

---

# H

## Hot Wash

An immediate post-exercise discussion where participants share observations, lessons, and initial improvement ideas while the experience is still fresh.

---

# I

## Improvement Action

A measurable task assigned to address a finding identified during an exercise or incident review.

---

## Incident

An event that threatens or adversely affects the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of information or systems.

---

## Incident Commander

The individual responsible for directing and coordinating the organization's incident response activities.

---

## Incident Response Plan (IRP)

A documented set of procedures describing how an organization prepares for, detects, responds to, and recovers from cybersecurity incidents.

---

## Indicator of Compromise (IOC)

Observable evidence suggesting a system or account may have been compromised, such as malicious IP addresses, unusual processes, suspicious files, or unauthorized account activity.

---

## Inject

A controlled event, message, artifact, or piece of information introduced during an exercise to stimulate participant discussion and decision-making.

---

# L

## Lessons Learned

Knowledge gained through exercise evaluation that contributes to organizational improvement.

---

## Lateral Movement

Techniques used by attackers to move between systems after gaining initial access.

---

# M

## Master Scenario Events List (MSEL)

The facilitator's detailed timeline describing exercise events, injects, expected participant actions, and scenario progression.

---

## Mean Time to Detect (MTTD)

The average time required to identify an incident after it begins.

---

## Mean Time to Recover (MTTR)

The average time required to restore normal operations following an incident.

---

## Mitigation

Actions that reduce the likelihood or impact of identified risks.

---

# N

## No-Blame Culture

An environment that encourages honest discussion and continuous improvement without assigning personal fault for exercise performance.

---

# O

## Observation

An objective record of participant behavior, discussion, or decision-making collected during an exercise.

Observations become findings only after analysis.

---

## Observer

An individual assigned to document participant actions and interactions without influencing the exercise.

---

# P

## Playbook

A documented, repeatable procedure for responding to a specific incident type or operational scenario.

---

## Recovery

The process of restoring systems, services, and business operations following containment and eradication.

---

## Recovery Point Objective (RPO)

The maximum acceptable amount of data loss measured in time.

---

## Recovery Time Objective (RTO)

The maximum acceptable duration required to restore a service or business process.

---

## Rules of Engagement (RoE)

The document defining exercise objectives, scope, boundaries, participant expectations, communication rules, and safety considerations.

---

# R

## Risk

The combination of the likelihood of an event and its potential consequences.

---

## Roadmap

A prioritized plan assigning owners, timelines, and validation activities for implementing improvements.

---

# S

## Scenario

A realistic narrative describing the fictional incident participants respond to during the exercise.

---

## Scope Creep

The gradual expansion of exercise objectives or boundaries beyond the original plan.

---

## Scribe

The individual responsible for documenting timelines, decisions, and significant discussion points during the exercise.

---

## Stakeholder

Any individual, team, department, regulator, customer, or partner affected by an exercise or incident.

---

# T

## Tabletop Exercise (TTX)

A structured, discussion-based exercise in which participants review a realistic scenario and describe how they would respond.

---

## Threat Intelligence

Information regarding adversaries, their capabilities, infrastructure, motivations, or techniques that supports informed decision-making.

---

## Timeline

A chronological record of exercise events, participant decisions, injects, and significant observations.

---

## Triage

The process of rapidly evaluating alerts or incidents to determine their severity, priority, and required response.

---

# V

## Validation

The process of confirming that an improvement, control, or corrective action has been successfully implemented and is functioning as intended.

---

# W

## White Cell

The planning and facilitation team responsible for controlling the exercise, introducing injects, answering participant questions, and maintaining scenario consistency.

# Maintaining the Glossary

As your exercise program matures, this glossary should evolve alongside it.

When introducing new technologies, regulatory requirements, frameworks, or organizational terminology:

* Add new terms alphabetically.
* Keep definitions concise and practical.
* Avoid duplicate or conflicting terminology.
* Cross-reference related concepts where appropriate.
* Review definitions periodically to ensure alignment with organizational practices.

A living glossary strengthens communication, reduces misunderstanding, and reinforces consistency across every tabletop exercise, workshop, and incident response activity.

Over time, this appendix can become the authoritative terminology reference for your organization's entire cybersecurity exercise program.
