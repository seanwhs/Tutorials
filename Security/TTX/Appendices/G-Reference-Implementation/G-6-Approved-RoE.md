# G.6 Rules of Engagement (Approved Exercise Version)

> *"The purpose of the Rules of Engagement is not to constrain discussion—it is to create a safe, consistent, and trusted environment in which meaningful discussion can occur."*

---

# Purpose

The Rules of Engagement (RoE) establish the operational framework governing Operation Red Horizon.

They define the exercise boundaries, participant expectations, communication standards, decision authorities, safety procedures, and facilitation principles required to conduct the exercise consistently and professionally.

All participants are expected to understand and comply with these Rules of Engagement before the exercise begins.

# Exercise Objectives

The Rules of Engagement support the following objectives:

* Promote constructive discussion.
* Ensure participant safety.
* Maintain scenario consistency.
* Encourage collaborative decision-making.
* Prevent confusion between exercise events and real-world operations.
* Capture meaningful organizational learning.

---

# Exercise Scope

The exercise covers the organizational response to a cybersecurity incident affecting multiple business functions.

The discussion includes:

* Incident detection.
* Incident response.
* Identity compromise.
* Third-party risk.
* Executive decision-making.
* Business continuity.
* Crisis communications.
* Recovery planning.

The exercise does **not** include technical validation or production changes.

---

# Exercise Boundaries

The following boundaries apply throughout the exercise.

Participants **may**:

* Discuss actions they would perform.
* Request additional information from the White Cell.
* Review exercise artifacts.
* Escalate issues according to organizational procedures.
* Challenge assumptions respectfully.

Participants **must not**:

* Access production systems.
* Execute technical response activities.
* Contact external organizations.
* Notify regulators or customers.
* Treat exercise communications as operational directives.
* Introduce facts that conflict with the approved scenario.

These boundaries preserve realism while preventing unintended operational consequences.

---

# No-Blame Principle

Operation Red Horizon is conducted under a formal no-blame philosophy.

The exercise exists to evaluate organizational processes rather than individual performance.

Participants are encouraged to:

* Ask questions.
* Explore alternative approaches.
* Admit uncertainty.
* Challenge assumptions.
* Discuss mistakes openly.
* Recommend improvements.

Observations collected during the exercise are used to improve organizational resilience—not to assess employee performance.

---

# Exercise Communications

All exercise communications must be clearly distinguishable from operational communications.

Approved prefixes include:

```text
[TTX-EXERCISE]
[EXERCISE ONLY]
[SIMULATION]
```

Recommended communication channels include:

* Dedicated collaboration channel.
* Exercise email distribution list.
* Facilitator announcements.
* White Cell communications.
* Exercise documentation repository.

No exercise messages should be transmitted through operational incident channels unless explicitly approved by the Exercise Director.

---

# Roles and Responsibilities

## Executive Sponsor

Provides executive oversight, approves objectives, and endorses organizational improvements arising from the exercise.

---

## Exercise Director

Maintains overall responsibility for governance, scope, schedule, and successful delivery.

The Exercise Director has final authority over exercise decisions.

---

## Lead Facilitator

Responsible for conducting the exercise.

Key responsibilities include:

* Introducing the scenario.
* Managing participant discussion.
* Maintaining pace.
* Encouraging balanced participation.
* Clarifying exercise assumptions.
* Coordinating with the White Cell.

---

## White Cell

The White Cell controls scenario execution.

Responsibilities include:

* Managing scenario progression.
* Delivering injects.
* Answering participant questions.
* Maintaining scenario consistency.
* Adjudicating participant requests.
* Adjusting exercise pacing where appropriate.

White Cell decisions are considered authoritative throughout the exercise.

---

## Scribe

Responsible for documenting:

* Decisions.
* Key discussion points.
* Escalations.
* Assumptions.
* Action items.
* Timeline events.

The exercise record produced by the Scribe forms an important input to the After Action Review.

---

## Observers

Observers monitor the exercise without participating in operational discussions.

Their responsibilities include documenting:

* Decision quality.
* Communication effectiveness.
* Coordination challenges.
* Process gaps.
* Organizational strengths.
* Improvement opportunities.

Observers should avoid influencing participant decisions unless specifically requested by the Exercise Director.

---

## Participants

Participants are expected to:

* Represent their assigned organizational roles.
* Make decisions consistent with organizational policy.
* Communicate openly.
* Respect differing viewpoints.
* Base decisions on available evidence.
* Explain significant decisions when requested.

---

# White Cell Authority

To preserve consistency, the White Cell may:

* Clarify scenario facts.
* Provide additional intelligence.
* Accelerate time.
* Delay injects.
* Modify exercise pacing.
* Introduce new developments.
* Simplify technical detail.
* Redirect discussion if required.

The White Cell will not intentionally mislead participants unless the learning objective specifically requires ambiguity.

# Information Requests

Participants may request additional information at any point.

Examples include:

* SIEM alerts.
* EDR findings.
* Identity logs.
* Firewall events.
* Business impact updates.
* Legal advice.
* Vendor communications.
* Executive briefings.

The White Cell determines whether requested information is:

* Already available.
* Reasonably obtainable.
* Outside exercise scope.
* Intentionally unavailable.

This reflects the uncertainty of real-world incident response.

---

# Pause and Stop Procedures

Two safety mechanisms are available throughout the exercise.

## PAUSE

A temporary suspension used to:

* Clarify misunderstandings.
* Resolve confusion.
* Address participant wellbeing.
* Correct administrative issues.

The exercise resumes once the issue has been resolved.

---

## STOP

An immediate termination of exercise activities.

STOP may be invoked if:

* A real cybersecurity incident occurs.
* A participant experiences significant distress.
* Sensitive operational information is inadvertently disclosed.
* Exercise activity interferes with business operations.
* The Exercise Director determines continuation is inappropriate.

The Exercise Director has final authority regarding STOP decisions.

---

# Artifact Handling

All exercise materials are fictional unless otherwise stated.

Participants must:

* Treat artifacts as confidential exercise material.
* Avoid sharing outside the exercise.
* Refrain from inserting real operational data.
* Use only sanitized examples.

Artifacts may include:

* Email messages.
* SIEM alerts.
* Executive updates.
* Help-desk transcripts.
* Media inquiries.
* Vendor communications.
* Screenshots.
* Network diagrams.

---

# Evaluation Philosophy

The exercise evaluates organizational capabilities rather than technical perfection.

Observers will assess:

* Collaboration.
* Leadership.
* Decision-making.
* Communication.
* Governance.
* Escalation.
* Risk management.
* Recovery planning.

There are no perfect answers.

The value of the exercise lies in thoughtful discussion and evidence-based decision-making.

---

# Expected Participant Behaviour

Participants are expected to:

* Respect all viewpoints.
* Listen actively.
* Avoid assigning blame.
* Challenge ideas rather than individuals.
* Focus on organizational improvement.
* Remain engaged throughout the session.
* Support collaborative problem-solving.

Professional conduct contributes directly to the quality of exercise outcomes.

These documents collectively form the official record of the exercise.
