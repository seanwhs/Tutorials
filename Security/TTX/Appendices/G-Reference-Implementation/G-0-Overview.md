# Introduction

Throughout this handbook, we have explored the principles, methodologies, tools, and practices required to design, facilitate, evaluate, and continuously improve cybersecurity tabletop exercises.

We have examined governance, exercise planning, threat modeling, Rules of Engagement, Master Scenario Events Lists (MSELs), facilitator techniques, participant management, artifact creation, observation methods, hot washes, After Action Reviews (AARs), and improvement roadmaps. Each chapter introduced a specific aspect of the tabletop exercise lifecycle, while the appendices provided reusable templates, checklists, sample artifacts, glossaries, and facilitator resources.

This appendix brings those individual components together into a single, cohesive implementation.

Rather than introducing new concepts, it demonstrates how the entire methodology can be applied to a realistic enterprise environment. Every document, timeline, inject, communication, and deliverable has been prepared as though it were part of an actual consulting engagement for a large multinational organization.

The result is a complete reference implementation that readers can study, adapt, and use as the foundation for their own tabletop exercise programs.

---

# Purpose

Operation Red Horizon serves four primary purposes.

First, it demonstrates how the concepts introduced throughout this handbook fit together into a coherent exercise package.

Second, it provides facilitators with a realistic example of an enterprise-scale tabletop exercise, including planning documentation, participant materials, facilitator guidance, exercise artifacts, and evaluation outputs.

Third, it illustrates how technical incident response, executive decision-making, business continuity, legal obligations, communications, and organizational governance intersect during a significant cybersecurity incident.

Finally, it provides a reusable blueprint that organizations can tailor to their own environments, industries, regulatory obligations, and threat landscapes.

# About Operation Red Horizon

Operation Red Horizon is a fictional—but realistic—cybersecurity incident involving a multinational manufacturing organization.

The scenario begins with the compromise of a trusted third-party vendor account used to provide remote support to manufacturing systems. Over the course of the exercise, participants investigate suspicious authentication activity, identify evidence of lateral movement, assess cloud and on-premises impacts, respond to ransomware deployment, coordinate executive communications, engage regulators and law enforcement, and ultimately oversee business recovery and organizational improvement.

The scenario has been deliberately designed to exercise both technical and non-technical stakeholders. While cybersecurity professionals investigate the attack, executive leaders must balance operational continuity, legal obligations, customer confidence, regulatory expectations, and reputational risk.

The objective is not to "win" the exercise by making perfect decisions. Rather, it is to strengthen organizational resilience by practising collaborative decision-making under realistic conditions of uncertainty.

# How to Use This Reference Implementation

Readers may use this appendix in several ways.

Those new to tabletop exercises may read it sequentially to understand how an enterprise exercise unfolds from planning through evaluation.

Experienced facilitators may use individual sections as reference material when developing their own scenarios, adapting injects, preparing facilitator guides, or conducting After Action Reviews.

Consultants may use the overall structure as a blueprint for client engagements, replacing the fictional organization with the client's business context while retaining the methodology and document flow.

Training organizations may also use Operation Red Horizon as the basis for instructor-led workshops, adapting the scenario to suit the maturity and objectives of each audience.

Regardless of how it is used, readers are encouraged to customize the materials rather than treating them as fixed documents. Every organization has unique business priorities, technologies, regulatory obligations, and risk tolerances. The greatest value comes from tailoring the framework presented here to reflect those realities.

# A Final Note Before We Begin

No two organizations respond to incidents in exactly the same way, and no tabletop exercise should attempt to predict every possible decision participants might make.

Operation Red Horizon is not intended to prescribe a single "correct" response. Instead, it provides one realistic example of how an enterprise-scale tabletop exercise can be planned, facilitated, documented, and evaluated using the principles described throughout this handbook.

As you work through the following sections, consider not only *what* decisions are made, but also *why* they are made, how they are communicated, what assumptions underpin them, and how they contribute to organizational learning.

With that foundation established, we now turn to the fictional organization at the centre of the exercise: **ACME Manufacturing Group**, whose business operations, technology landscape, and strategic priorities provide the backdrop for Operation Red Horizon.
