# Appendix F — Facilitator's Inject Cookbook

# Part 5 — Executive Pressure, Media & Regulatory Escalation

> *Technology incidents become business crises when trust is at stake. Once customers, regulators, investors, employees, or the media become involved, every decision carries operational, legal, financial, and reputational consequences.*

By this stage of the exercise, participants should already understand the technical aspects of the incident. The focus now shifts to leadership, governance, communications, and stakeholder management.

Unlike earlier phases, there are rarely purely technical solutions. Participants must make decisions while balancing transparency, legal obligations, business continuity, customer confidence, and organizational reputation.

---

# Learning Objectives

These injects assess the organization's ability to:

* Brief executive leadership.
* Support Board oversight.
* Coordinate public communications.
* Manage regulatory reporting.
* Engage law enforcement.
* Respond to media scrutiny.
* Handle investor concerns.
* Maintain stakeholder confidence.

# Category 22 — Executive Leadership

---

# Inject EL-01 — CEO Requests a Five-Minute Brief

## Objective

Evaluate executive communication.

### Difficulty

L2

### Timing

T+120

### Delivery

Phone call

### Scenario

The CEO is boarding an international flight and requests a five-minute verbal briefing before losing connectivity.

### Expected Discussion

Participants should communicate:

* What is confirmed.
* What remains uncertain.
* Current business impact.
* Recommended executive decisions.

### Facilitator Notes

Encourage concise, business-focused communication. Avoid excessive technical terminology.

---

# Inject EL-02 — Emergency Executive Committee Meeting

## Objective

Assess cross-functional coordination.

### Scenario

The Executive Committee assembles within thirty minutes.

Each executive has different priorities:

* COO: Restore operations.
* CFO: Assess financial exposure.
* General Counsel: Preserve legal privilege.
* CISO: Continue containment.
* HR: Inform employees.
* Communications Director: Prepare external messaging.

### Discussion

How should competing priorities be reconciled?

---

The Chair of the Board requests:

## Objective

Exercise external communications.

### Scenario

## Objective

Evaluate monitoring and communications.

### Scenario

Posts begin appearing on multiple social media platforms claiming:

* customer data has been stolen,
* systems are offline,
* employees cannot work.

### Facilitator Notes

Participants should distinguish between rumours and confirmed information.

# Facilitator Escalation Techniques

At this stage, pressure should arise primarily from:

* time,
* uncertainty,
* stakeholder expectations,
* competing priorities,
* incomplete evidence.

Avoid introducing unrealistic drama.

The scenario should remain credible and representative of real organizational crises.

# Facilitator Best Practices

When role-playing external stakeholders:

* Remain professional and credible.
* Ask realistic questions.
* Avoid creating "gotcha" moments.
* Reward participants who acknowledge uncertainty appropriately.
* Encourage evidence-based communication rather than speculation.

A well-run exercise should reinforce that trust is built through honesty, consistency, and disciplined decision-making—not by having immediate answers to every question.

---

# Design Principles

The communications phase is often where organizations experience the greatest pressure. Technical teams seek certainty, while executives, customers, regulators, and the media expect timely decisions despite incomplete information.

These injects encourage participants to practice balancing speed with accuracy, transparency with legal obligations, and operational recovery with long-term organizational reputation.

Success should be measured not by perfect messaging, but by thoughtful governance, coordinated leadership, and the ability to maintain stakeholder confidence throughout an evolving crisis.
